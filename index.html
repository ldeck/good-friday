Good News or Good Riddance?
Roman & Jewish historians · c. 93–121 AD
"They affirmed… that they met on a certain fixed day before it was light, sang in alternate verses a hymn to Christ as to a god, bound themselves by a solemn oath not to commit fraud, theft, or adultery… and then reassembled to partake of food of an ordinary and innocent kind."
— Pliny the Younger, Letters X.96 — same letter — on what the torture actually uncovered
"I interrogated them… those who persisted I ordered executed… I thought it necessary to find out what the truth was by torturing two female slaves… but I found nothing more than a depraved, excessive superstition."

"Christus… suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus, and a most mischievous superstition, thus checked for the moment, again broke out not only in Judæa, the first source of the evil, but even in Rome."
— Tacitus, Annals XV.44, Roman senator & historian (c. 116 AD), on Nero's persecution c. 64 AD
"Pilate condemned him to be crucified… those who had first loved him did not cease."
— Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews XVIII.3.3, Jewish historian (c. 93–94 AD)
"Claudius expelled the Jews from Rome, since they were constantly making disturbances at the instigation of Chrestus."
— Suetonius, Life of Claudius 25.4, Roman historian (c. 121 AD), on events c. 49 AD

With Good Friday coming up, I’ve always found the name a bit curious… what exactly is “good” about it?

Outer layer – Later Roman perspectives

Pliny the Younger, governor of Bithynia-Pontus (c. 111–113 AD), to Emperor Trajan:
“I interrogated them… those who persisted I ordered executed… others I had led away… to be punished. I thought it necessary to find out what the truth was by torturing two female slaves… but I found nothing more than a depraved, excessive superstition.”
“They affirmed… that they met on a certain fixed day before it was light, sang in alternate verses a hymn to Christ as to a god, bound themselves by a solemn oath not to commit fraud, theft, or adultery… and then reassembled to partake of food of an ordinary and innocent kind.”

Tacitus, Roman senator and historian (c. 116 AD), on Christians in Rome under Nero (reigned 54–68 AD):
“Christus… suffered the extreme penalty at the hands of Pontius Pilate… and a most mischievous superstition, thus checked for the moment, again broke out… not only in Judea… but even in Rome.”

Suetonius (c. 121 AD), Roman historian, on Claudius (reigned 41–54 AD):
“He expelled the Jews from Rome, since they were constantly making disturbances at the instigation of Chrestus.”

Josephus, Jewish historian (c. 93–94 AD):
“Now there was about this time Jesus, a wise man… Pilate condemned him to be crucified… those who had first loved him did not cease.”

Middle layer – Early opposition in Jerusalem (Acts 7–9, c. 30–34 AD)

Reaction to Stephen’s speech
Council members’ anger and action, Sanhedrin, Jerusalem:
“They were furious and gnashed their teeth at him. They dragged him out of the city and began to stone him… The witnesses laid their coats at the feet of a young man named Saul.” (Acts 7:54–58)

Stephen’s speech to the Sanhedrin explaining the cause of their anger:
“You stiff-necked people! You always resist the Holy Spirit… Which of the prophets did your ancestors not persecute?” (Acts 7:51–52)

Saul’s threats against the movement (after Stephen’s death, c. 34 AD)
Acts 9:1–2, Jerusalem and surrounding synagogues:
“Meanwhile, Saul was still breathing out murderous threats against the Lord’s disciples. He went to the high priest and asked him for letters to the synagogues in Damascus, so that if he found any who belonged to the Way, whether men or women, he might take them as prisoners to Jerusalem.”

Men in Jerusalem, supporters of Saul, took an oath:
“They bound themselves under an oath not to eat or drink until they had killed him.” (Acts 9:1–2)

Roman centurions / guards around Jerusalem at this time (Acts 22:24–29):
“The commander ordered him to be struck on the mouth… The tribune heard this and intervened, asking if the man was a Roman citizen… When he learned it, he became afraid, because he had put a Roman citizen in chains.”

Saul’s conversion (flashback, Acts 9:3–6):
“As he neared Damascus on his journey, suddenly a light from heaven flashed around him. He fell to the ground and heard a voice say, ‘Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting me?’ ‘Who are you, Lord?’ Saul asked. ‘I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting.’ ‘Now get up and go into the city, and you will be told what you must do.’”

Apostles commanded by the high priest and associates (Sanhedrin, Jerusalem, Acts 5:28–29, key sentences):
“We gave you strict orders not to teach in this name.”
“We cannot help speaking about what we have seen and heard… We must obey God rather than men.”

Inner layer – Jesus’ ministry, Last Supper, trial, and crucifixion (c. early April 30 AD, Jerusalem)

Journey to Jerusalem (Luke 18:31–33):
“We are going up to Jerusalem… the Son of Man will be delivered over… they will condemn him to death… and on the third day he will rise.”

Last Supper / Passover meal (Luke 22:19–20):
“While they were eating, Jesus took bread… ‘This is my body given for you; do this in remembrance of me.’ In the same way, after the supper he took the cup, saying, ‘This cup is the new covenant in my blood, which is poured out for you.’”

Trial and crucifixion
Caiaphas, high priest:
    > “It is better for you that one man die for the people than that the whole nation perish.” (John 11:50)
The crowd:
    > “He saved others; let him save himself if he is the Christ of God.” (Luke 23:35)
Pontius Pilate, governor of Judea:
    > “I find no basis for a charge against him… What shall I do, then, with the one you call king?” (Matthew 27:22–23)

Flashbacks to Jesus’ earlier statements during trial/execution:
“The Son of Man came… to give his life as a ransom for many.” (Mark 10:45)
“Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing.” (Luke 23:34)
“It is finished.” (John 19:30)

From the outside—executions, political calculations, violent threats, and a movement labeled “mischievous”—the question of what is “good” appears to have many sides.

And yet, those same events led some to worship Christ, to change how they lived, and eventually to call it Good Friday.
